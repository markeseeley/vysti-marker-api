Wonder is the process of making an observation, becoming progressively curious, and eventually leading towards questions and thoughts. Wonder could also be the feeling of surprise or amazement. As Neel Burton notes, Francis Bacon suggested in the early 17th century that the term 'wonder' may find its source in the German Wunde (wound), giving a sense of a "breach [that] yearns to be filled" (Psychology Today). In other words, wonder leads to the acquisition of knowledge by revealing a gap in understanding and a desire to fill that gap. However, to gain knowledge, we need to first be curious about a topic; otherwise, we would not be inclined to discover more, and therefore we would not acquire knowledge.

While wonder exists in many fields of study, for the purpose of this essay, the focus will be on the natural sciences and the arts. In the natural sciences, we are curious about how the universe functions, and how to apply that understanding in real-world applications. Meanwhile, in the visual arts, we typically encounter wonder through our senses and imagination from what we view and feel in visual arts. Acquiring knowledge does not destroy our sense of wonder, rather, wonder is amplified through the desire to discover new knowledge.

Oftentimes, in the natural sciences, knowers encounter knowledge that is incredibly vast and mysterious, leading to a sense of wonder through comparison of magnitude. When referring to magnitude, this can signify size and complexity, which both can enhance wonder, as something massive or minuscule may seem mysterious. This is exemplified by the universe, as it is largely unexplored and leaves a sense of mystery and curiosity, leading knowers to compare humanity with the universe. However, it may be challenging to understand a subject that is too large, small, or complex. Due to this, scientists have found indirect methods of comprehending a subject. For example, in the study of the origins of the universe in physics, the acquisition of knowledge does not appear to destroy wonder. In 1933,

Fritz Zwicky presented evidence for dark matter, coining the term dunkle Materie, and his theory was later confirmed in the 1970s by Vera Rubin (Manuel Bautista). What was learned is that dark matter makes up 27% of the universe, while normal matter makes up only 5% (NASA). Additionally, dark matter is invisible, as it does not emit, absorb, or reflect light, its existence is known due to its gravitational interactions with visible matter (NASA). Knowers can only wonder about dark matter due to the knowledge that was obtained from developing knowledge of the universe, the planets, and the stars. Without this knowledge, we would not have discovered the existence of dark matter. As dark matter makes up a proportionate amount of mass in the universe, the knower immediately compares the minuteness of humanity on Earth in the context of the magnitude of space. The sense of humanity being minuscule and insignificant amplifies our curiosity about the universe and raises questions about life beyond the solar system and how phenomena in the universe work. Furthermore, very little is still known about dark matter, as scientists can only utilise indirect methods and evidence to prove its existence, like algorithms, artificial intelligence, and quantum computers (Manuel Bautista). These indirect methods used by the scientific community serve to increase wonder, as we currently don't have any direct way to experiment and continuously learn about dark matter, still leaving the origins of the universe shrouded in mystery. From this example, it can be seen that when an area of knowledge is so vast that it cannot be comprehended by a single knower, resulting in a persistent mystery surrounding the object of knowledge, producing a sense of wonder.

In the world of visual arts, acquiring knowledge allows the knower to envision how components unify and maintain order. Wonder is found in wholeness, especially in how a subject's different components unify and contain order. When acquiring knowledge, there are still many phenomena that remain incomprehensible, like dark matter. To understand and

process these phenomena, we are forced to take a part of the whole, analyse that part and apply it to the whole. However, wonder in the visual arts is produced through a reciprocal relationship between the part and the whole, where to comprehend the part, the knower must see it as part of a larger whole, in which this whole becomes increasingly vast. This is demonstrated by Chuck Close, an artist who creates photorealistic portraits made up of smaller, abstract shapes.

When taking a look at the abstract pieces of the artwork, the viewer may not understand what each individual piece is. However, if the viewer looks at all the pieces of the artwork together, it provides the viewer with the whole context of the painting. Furthermore, this curiosity could be further extended to the artists themselves. This further illustrates how the part is related to the whole, as the artwork itself is a part of the artist, which is considered a whole. Like the abstract pieces of the artwork, the artwork interacts with different parts of the artist's identity, such as their passions and interests, the artistic techniques they used, their own artistic style, the reasons for choosing this method, and much more. This may further pique the viewer's interest and wonder, triggering more curiosity and leading to acquiring knowledge of the artist and their artworks. This also further demonstrates how the relationship between acquiring knowledge and the creation, instead of the destruction of wonder, is a cycle. This is because as we wonder, we may feel the urge to acquire knowledge, and when we acquire knowledge, we may discover new details that we have not observed beforehand, leading us to admire it and to search for the answers to new questions, (and so this repeats continuously.) This also allows us to develop a deeper understanding, perspective, and connection to the surrounding world and other individuals, as art creates a bridge between personal and shared knowledge. As seen in the visual arts, knowing what the part does concerning the whole allows the knower to see the complex interactions, leading to a heightened sense of wonder.

In contrast to vast and complex subjects, there are some aspects of knowledge that are simplified, resulting in the destruction of wonder. In other words, oversimplification destroys wonder. Such an example could be seen in the reductionist perspective in biology, as simply reducing organisms to chemical components removes the wonder of the complexity within an organism. Although 99% of the human body is fundamentally comprised of six elements—

carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, calcium, and phosphorus (Davey). By stating what essentially humans consist of, it removes the closely intertwined relationships and complexity between the different internal systems of the human body and these necessary molecules, removing the curiosity and wonder we may have about the functions and relationships between different body systems. Not only that but by simplifying organisms to their most basic molecules, it also neglects important details in how organisms function and what makes different living species unique and different. Reductionism also attenuates wonder through the removal of interactions between molecules themselves and the environment, which is also made of atoms. Moreover, simplifying the critical roles of these basic molecules undermines their importance in maintaining the processes of life, like metabolism, homeostasis, and respiration. Additionally, this oversimplified explanation lacks depth and complexity, which means that the process of acquiring knowledge of the simplified explanation of a biological process or system can be unsatisfactory, therefore destroying the knower's sense of wonder.

The arguments and supporting evidence exemplify how the sense of wonder is expanded upon after acquiring knowledge in the Areas of Knowledge, such as the natural sciences and the arts. Through the analysis of the arguments, it has become clear that there are still mysteries that leave us wondering, leading to more questions than answers. On one hand, it has also become increasingly clear that the natural sciences aim to find answers through standardised procedures due to scientists' wonder of the world, discovering new things that we may have not previously observed. On the other hand, the arts aim to create wonder by visualising and illustrating an individual's own human experience. However, even outside of the fields of the natural sciences and the arts, we find wonder in many other aspects of the world as well. Moreover, the methods used to lead to these discoveries are often indirect, as there is no direct way to completely understand the universe, or know the

thought process of an artist while they are painting. Therefore, as demonstrated by evidence from the natural sciences and the arts, wonder is not destroyed by knowledge acquisition, it is rather amplified through our desire to acquire knowledge.
